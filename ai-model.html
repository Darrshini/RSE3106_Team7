How T.O.P.H Sees the Crossing

A single YOLO11 segmentation model finds the crossing's dotted lines and the pedestrian light. Classical computer vision then reads the light's colour and works out which way to walk — turning a camera into three spoken answers: cross now, walk this way, and you've reached the other side.

01

The Segmentation Model

T.O.P.H runs on one vision model: a YOLO11 instance-segmentation network that outlines the crossing's dashed markings and locates the pedestrian light. Everything else — the light's colour, the walking direction, the sense of having arrived — is classical computer vision layered on top of the model's detections.

It was transfer-learned from COCO-pretrained YOLO11s-seg (the small variant, chosen for real-time inference on modest hardware) and exported to ONNX for deployment.

Collection Team-captured iPhone photos of Singapore crossings; two Roboflow projects merged by class name — 633 images total.
Labelling Polygon masks (instance segmentation) for dotted line — the crossing-corridor markings — and pedestrian light.
Pre-processing Merged to a shared taxonomy in YOLO-seg format; letterboxed to 640px; heavy domain-gap augmentation to match the deployment camera — motion blur, softening, desaturation, sensor noise, JPEG compression — because training shots were crisp iPhone but the wearable uses a Raspberry Pi video feed.
Backbone YOLO11s-seg (small), fine-tuned from COCO weights. Outputs bounding box + 32 mask coefficients + 160x160 prototypes.
Training Single-GPU transfer learning; letterbox 640, patience 25 early-stopping, recipe tuned for the augmented set; exported to ONNX (opset 12).
Result Box mAP@50 0.86, mask mAP@50 0.89 on clean frames — 0.84 / 0.81 on Pi-degraded frames.
02

The Colour Model That Didn't Work

The first attempt was a plain colour detector — a YOLO11 model trained to spot the light as red, green, or traffic-light. It answered only one question (what colour?), and answered it unreliably: the light-housing class scored just AP 0.44, and colour classification struggled in night and low-resolution frames.

Two realisations drove the change. First, a blind pedestrian needs geometry the colour model never produced — which way to walk, and when the far kerb is reached. Second, colour is read more reliably by classical HSV on a detected light box than by a learned 3-class detector. So the colour model was dropped and replaced by the segmentation model, which supplies the crossing geometry while classical CV handles the colour.

03

The CV That Does the Guiding

The model finds what and where; these three routines turn detections into spoken guidance.

Colour
Reading the Light

Classical HSV on the detected light box counts lit pixels. Green-present and red-present are reported independently, making the blink readable: green only = cross; green + red = clearance (wait); red only is held as still-green until it persists 2+ seconds. A timer bridges the 500ms on/off blink so it never flickers to red.

Direction
The Vanishing-Point Vector

Heading comes from the segmentation masks, not the light. PCA finds each dashed line's dominant axis; the two boundary lines converge in perspective at a vanishing point — that's where the crossing leads. The spoken direction runs from the pedestrian's feet toward the vanishing point: the corridor centreline to the far kerb.

Arrival
Knowing You've Crossed

Detected by dashes-run-out + light-proximity: when the dotted lines are no longer segmented ahead for several consecutive frames and the pedestrian light has grown large or dropped out of view, the pedestrian has crossed. A hardware IMU/GPS fail-safe is planned for future iterations.
