Our Journey

Design Iterations

Every Decision Had a Reason

Each major pivot was driven by a specific failure we discovered during testing — here's the engineering story behind T.O.P.H.

Form Factor
Smart Cane → Smart Shoe → Smart Spectacle

Smart canes were rejected — the market is saturated and users prefer lightweight traditional canes. Smart shoes failed on camera placement (below hip, obstructed view) and privacy concerns. The spectacle puts the camera at eye-level for unobstructed, privacy-safe AI detection.

App Platform
Native Android → Browser Web App

Every native code change required a full compile-sign-deploy cycle. With less than 2 months to build both hardware and software, the web app let us push and test instantly — WebSocket, DeviceOrientation, and Web Speech all worked cross-device with no platform-specific code.

Audio
Dedicated Speaker Module → Phone Built-In Audio

A separate audio module meant more hardware, another Bluetooth pairing step, and extra weight on the glasses. We routed all feedback through Web Speech Synthesis on the user's own phone — zero additional pairing, zero extra hardware, controls they already know.

AI Processing
On-Device AI → Cloud-Hosted on AWS EC2

Real-time inference competed for the same battery and CPU the phone needed for camera streaming and speech. We split the workload: heavy AI on EC2, lightweight capture on the phone. When our t2.micro got OOM-killed, we resized to t3.small — one server change, zero client changes.

Camera
ESP32 → Raspberry Pi

The ESP32 failed all three tests: images pixelated at 10m, motion caused total blur (no autofocus), and thermal throttling dropped video from 30fps to under 1fps within minutes. The Pi boosted AI detection confidence from ~70% to ~94%, at a +10% weight cost we accepted as mandatory.

Localisation
Google Places API → Overpass / OpenStreetMap

Google Places returned wrong locations because traffic_signals isn't a valid Places type — it's a business directory, not infrastructure data. Overpass API queries real OSM crossing tags, with three mirror endpoints and a camera-only YOLO fallback if no data is returned.

Hardware Design
Spectacle V1 (Compact) → V2 (Expanded)

V1 was sized for the ESP32's smaller footprint. After the mandatory Pi upgrade, V2 redesigned the chassis with expanded cavities for the larger board, proper thermal clearance, and passive airflow — accepting +5% size for a step-change in detection quality.

Processing
60fps → 1 Frame per 0.15s (~7fps)

Our CPU-only AWS instance took ~150ms per frame — at 60fps, a massive backlog crashed the system. We sample 1 frame per 0.15s, matching CPU capacity. At walking speed (1.4 m/s), this captures movement in ~21cm increments — enough for safe pedestrian detection.

Current
Demo-Ready Prototype

13-state FSM, gesture recognition, Web Speech Synthesis, dual-mode camera input, double-confirmation safety gates, and stall-detection crossing timer — field-tested and submitted for the James Dyson Award.

T.O.P.H

Traffic Optical Personal Helper — real-time crossing assistance for visually impaired pedestrians.
